My process

  1. Strategy

    Often times I see designers and project teams jumping right into design and development without enough consideration for a design strategy. At one point in my career, I fit this description. However, as I have grown I have seen the importance of strategy. Before starting a project, I like to consider all stakeholders and encourage them to break out of their segmented thinking. If possible, I like to use design sprints and working sessions to learn about the different users, applications involved, the architecture and any other relevant information. Strategy sessions have helped me to generate conceptual designs and prototypes as well as shedding light on areas where we may need to conduct research and analysis.

  2. Research & analysis

    User research & analysis is an extremely important input to my design process. Understanding a user's behavior and mental model has allowed me to create designs focused on the user's actual needs and not just their perceived ones. Performing contextual interviews, observing users, and competitor analysis are all effective methods of user research that I have used to inform my designs.

  3. Design & ideation

    Starting with a pen and paper is always my preferred method when generating ideas for a complex workflow. While working on commercial insurance applications, I often encounter complex business workflows, and writing out all the possible scenarios helps me to organize my thoughts and consider the workflow without fixating on a solution too early. Once I have some high level conceptual sketches, I will work to develop some more detailed designs and prototypes. I mainly work with Sketch, inVision, and Axure RP. Peer review is extremely important for me, and I always look for feedback from my fellow designers and front-end developers. I will share my designs with engineers and key business partners involved in the project to gain buy-in. Once everyone involved has agreed on the design, I will polish up the design or prototype and prepare it to share with the engineer building the feature.

  4. Development

    Working with developers is crucial to being a good user experience designer, and understanding front-end development has been a great tool for me. Once the designs have been finished, I will pass the specs over to the engineer, but the work simply cannot stop there. Follow up conversations are always needed. I often find myself working with developers to implement the solution and to make adjustments while development is happening. Things don't always go as planned, so making sure that the lines of communication are open with the engineering team is a must do.

  5. Usability testing & evaluation

    Testing designs with actual users is arguably the most important part of user-experience and user-centered design. I can design something aesthetically beautiful that makes sense to the entire project team, but until I get it in front of an actual user, I don't know if the design works. Validating workflows with users is an integral part of my design process. I write test scripts and perform task-based analysis of my designs with users to gain insight into their thought process while they use the application. I ask users to think out loud while I probe about workflows that I want to get feedback on. After testing (usually with 5 users), I analyze the results and compile reports with my design recommendations. Testing before releasing features to production allows us to make updates for better workflows and designs before users struggle and get frustrated.
